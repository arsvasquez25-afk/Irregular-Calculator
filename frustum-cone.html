Frustum of a Cone

A cone with its pointed top sliced off by a flat horizontal cut, leaving two circular faces of different sizes.

🔺 What is a Frustum of a Cone?

Imagine a regular ice cream cone. Now slice off the pointed top with a straight horizontal cut. What remains is a frustum of a cone — a 3D shape with a large circular base at the bottom and a smaller circular base at the top, connected by a slanted side.


The word "frustum" comes from Latin meaning "morsel cut off." It is one of the most common shapes in everyday engineering and construction.

📐 The Formula

V = (1/3) × π × h × (R² + R·r + r²)
VariableMeaning
VVolume — the space inside the frustum
hHeight — the vertical distance between the two circular bases
RBig Radius — radius of the larger bottom circle
rSmall Radius — radius of the smaller top circle
πPi — the constant 3.14159…

🧠 How the Formula Works (Step by Step)

1

Start with the expression inside the parentheses: (R² + R·r + r²). This accounts for the changing circular cross-section — from the big base R, through the middle transition R·r, down to the small top r.

2

Multiply by π (pi) to bring in the circular nature of the shape. Circles always involve π.

3

Multiply by h (the height) to stretch the cross-section into a 3D solid.

4

Multiply by 1/3 because the shape tapers (like a cone), so it holds only one-third the volume of a full cylinder of the same height.

🌍 Real-Life Uses

🪣

Buckets and Pails

A standard plastic bucket is wider at the top and narrower at the bottom — a perfect frustum of a cone. Engineers use this formula to calculate exactly how many liters it holds.

🏗️

Cooling Towers

Large industrial cooling towers (like those in power plants) are frustum-shaped. Engineers calculate their internal volume to determine air and water flow capacity.

🚦

Traffic Cones

Traffic cones are frustums. Manufacturers calculate the volume of rubber or plastic material needed to produce each cone in bulk.
