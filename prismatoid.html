The Prismatoid

The most general of the three — a solid with two parallel bases of any shape and size, connected by flat or curved faces, with a measurable middle cross-section.

📐 What is a Prismatoid?

A prismatoid is the most flexible of the irregular solids. It is defined as any solid that has two parallel bases (which can be any polygon, circle, or irregular shape) and whose lateral faces are triangles, trapezoids, or parallelograms.


What makes it special is the middle cross-section (Am) — an imaginary slice taken exactly halfway between the two bases. This middle area is key to the formula. Prisms, pyramids, cones, and frustums are all special cases of a prismatoid.

📐 The Formula (Prismoidal / Simpson's Rule)

V = (h/6) × (A1 + A2 + 4 × Am)
VariableMeaning
VVolume — total space inside the prismatoid
hHeight — total vertical distance between the two bases
A1Area of the bottom base
A2Area of the top base
AmArea of the middle cross-section (a slice exactly at h/2)
4 × AmThe middle is weighted 4× because it has the greatest influence on the volume

🧠 How the Formula Works (Step by Step)

1

Measure the bottom area A1 and the top area A2. These are the two ends of the solid.

2

Find the middle cross-section Am — cut the solid exactly in half (at height h/2) and measure the area of that slice. This is the most important measurement.

3

Compute A1 + A2 + 4·Am. The middle section is multiplied by 4 because it represents the bulk of the volume — this is borrowed from Simpson's Rule in calculus.

4

Multiply by h/6. Dividing by 6 correctly balances the weighted sum into an accurate volume regardless of the shape of the bases.

🌍 Real-Life Uses

⛰️

Earthwork and Land Surveying

Civil engineers use the prismatoid formula to estimate the volume of soil to be cut or filled when constructing roads, dams, and embankments. The two bases are the start and end cross-sections, and Am is the middle cross-section of the terrain.

🏊

Swimming Pools and Reservoirs

Irregularly shaped pools that are wider at one end and narrower at the other — with varying depth — are modeled as prismatoids. Engineers calculate water capacity using this formula.

🌉

Bridge Abutments

The concrete supports at the ends of a bridge are often prismatoid-shaped. Structural engineers use this formula to calculate the exact volume of concrete needed during construction.
